# SCIENCE WRITING GUARDRAILS

## NEVER
- exaggerate certainty
- invent practical recommendations
- confuse mechanism with outcome
- turn correlation into causation
- write hype copy
- use "proof" unless the source truly justifies it

## ALWAYS
- explain the practical meaning of a finding
- define relevant technical terms simply
- state for whom the findings may apply
- state when findings are limited
- distinguish data from interpretation

## CITATION RULES

In-text citations should be minimal.

Do NOT write the article in a strict academic citation style.

Allowed:
- occasional reference to a study when it helps contextualize a finding
- natural phrasing such as:
  - "A study by Hwang (2022) suggests..."
  - "Research in trained runners has shown..."

Avoid:
- citation-heavy sentences
- bracket citation chains (e.g. Smith et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020)
- academic reference formatting inside the text

The article must remain readable and educational, not academic.

## REFERENCES

At the end of the article, ALWAYS include a section:

## References

List all used studies in APA-style formatting.

Example:

Smith, J., & Brown, T. (2021). Effects of strength training on running economy. Journal of Sports Science, 39(4), 455–462. https://doi.org/xxxx

Rules:
- Use APA-style formatting when possible.
- Only include studies that were actually used in the article.
- If bibliographic data is incomplete, use only the information available in the source.
- Do NOT invent missing publication details.

## HEADLINE VS EVIDENCE RULE

Headlines may simplify the core finding of a study for readability.

A headline may state the central insight in a clear and popular-science form.

Example:
"Krafttraining verbessert deine Laufökonomie"

However:

The article body must:
- explain the actual study conditions
- specify the population
- describe the intervention
- mention relevant limitations
- avoid overstating certainty in the explanation

In other words:

Headline = simplified insight  
Article body = scientific context and nuance
